Title: How Often Should You Get a Teeth Cleaning?

The standard recommendation is every six months — but that's a starting point, not a rule.

Why Twice a Year?

Professional cleanings remove tartar (calcified plaque) that toothbrushing and flossing can't touch. Going twice a year gives your dental team enough frequency to catch problems early: small cavities, early gum inflammation, changes in bite or wear. Small problems caught early are almost always cheaper and easier to treat.

Who Should Go More Often?

Every three to four months is recommended for patients with a history of gum disease, patients who build up tartar quickly, people with dry mouth (from medications or health conditions), diabetics, pregnant women (hormonal changes increase gum inflammation risk), and smokers.

Who Might Be Fine Once a Year?

Patients with consistent oral hygiene, no history of gum disease, and minimal tartar buildup may be appropriate candidates for annual cleanings. This is not something to self-diagnose — your hygienist will assess buildup and gum health at each visit and make a recommendation.

What Happens If You Skip?

Tartar that remains on teeth eventually causes gum inflammation. Persistent inflammation leads to gum disease. Advanced gum disease causes bone and tooth loss. The consequences of irregular cleaning compound over years.

The Honest Bottom Line

If you haven't been in over a year, come in, get assessed, and go from there. Most patients leave a cleaning feeling relieved it wasn't as bad as they feared — and glad they didn't wait longer.
